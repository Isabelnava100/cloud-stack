Frequently Asked Questions

A surety bond is a three-party agreement that guarantees the performance of a contract or adherence to regulations. It involves the principal (the party required to obtain the bond), the obligee (the party receiving the bond benefit), and the surety (the company issuing the bond). Surety bonds are commonly used in construction, licensing, and legal matters to ensure that obligations are met. For example, an SC Mechanical Contractor Bond ensures that a contractor will fulfill their contractual obligations according to the terms agreed upon.

An SC Mechanical Contractor Bond ensures that a mechanical contractor complies with state regulations and fulfills contractual obligations. When a contractor obtains this bond, they are backed by a surety company that guarantees their performance. If the contractor fails to meet the terms of the contract or violates regulations, the injured party can file a claim against the bond. The surety company will investigate and, if the claim is valid, compensate the claimant up to the bond amount. The contractor is then responsible for reimbursing the surety for any claims paid.
